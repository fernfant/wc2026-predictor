How to predict the

A small, transparent model that turns team strength into match odds, then plays the whole 48-team tournament 20,000 times to find each team's chance of lifting the trophy. Every assumption is a knob you can turn.

2 · The model, in three steps

strength → goals → scoreline

Step 1 — Strength is an Elo number

Every team starts with a single number from the World Football Elo system — Argentina 2144, Spain 2134, … Haiti 1517. Then two knobs adjust it into an effective rating:

eff = elo + (host ? home_adv : concacaf_bonus) + form_weight × form

That's the home-advantage and recent-form levers — the two things a rating alone misses.

Step 2 — The gap becomes expected goals

The rating gap feeds a log-linear model. An even match means each side expects avg_goals (≈1.30); the stronger team's expectation grows exponentially with the gap:

Δ = (eff_A − eff_B) / 400
λ_A = 1.30 × e^(β·Δ) λ_B = 1.30 × e^(−β·Δ)

Step 3 — Goals become a scoreline

Goals arrive at random, so we draw each team's goals from a Poisson distribution around its λ. That gives a concrete scoreline — and therefore a win, draw, or loss, plus the goals-for/against that decide group tiebreakers. Run it once for a result; run it 20,000 times for probabilities.

P(k goals) = e^(−λ) · λ^k / k!
Why hybrid? Elo is great at ranking strength but says nothing about scorelines. Poisson turns strength into goals — which you need for group standings (GD, GF) and for the drama of a 2–1. Together they give both odds and scores.

3 · Fatigue: age and squad depth

old legs tire, deep benches don't — and it compounds each round

A pure rating treats a team as equally fresh in the final as in its opener. Real squads don't work that way. By the semi-finals a side has played six high-intensity games in three weeks — and who copes depends on two things about the individual players: how old they are, and how much quality sits on the bench to rotate in.

So each squad carries two numbers (in data/teams.csv): its real average age (a summary of every player called up) and a depth rating from 1 (one thin XI) to 5 (two strong XIs). Fatigue is an Elo penalty that grows every round, amplified by age and softened by depth:

penalty = fatigue_per_match × matches_played × age_mult × depth_mult
age_mult  = 1 + 0.10 × (avg_age − 27.5) # older → bigger
depth_mult = 1 − 0.15 × (depth − 3) # deeper → smaller

Crucially the penalty is applied to both teams, so between two similar squads it cancels. It only swings a match when an old, thin team meets a young, deep one — and only late, once matches_played is large. The group stage is unaffected.

Turning fatigue on flips the favourite: Spain (26.2, depth 5) edges past Argentina (28.6) for the pre-tournament #1, 13.5% to 13.3% — the title-race chart below already includes it. Use the tournament-stage selector in the live demo above to watch an old/thin side bleed Elo as the rounds pile up.

data Average ages are real (RotoWire, all 48 squads); depth is an editable 1–5 estimate of bench strength. Both live in the CSV — change them and re-run.

5 · Playing the whole tournament

12 groups → best thirds → knockout → champion

One simulation of the cup:

  1. Groups — 12 groups of 4, round-robin. Rank by points → goal difference → goals scored.
  2. Qualify — top 2 of each group, plus the 8 best third-placed teams → 32 teams.
  3. Knockout — the official FIFA round-of-32 bracket (with the third-place allocation table) runs through to the final. Level games go to a skill-weighted shootout.
  4. Repeat — do all of that 20,000 times and count how often each team reaches each round.

7 · Fine-tuning on the real group stage

calibrate.py — fit the principles to what actually happened

The group stage is over: 72 matches, 215 goals, 2.99 per game. That's a fresh batch of evidence — so we can retrofit the model. Holding the pre-tournament Elo ratings fixed, we search for the principle values that best reproduce every team's actual goals-for, goals-against and points across its three games (minimising squared error).

The real group stage was more lopsided than the defaults expected — France 10–2, Germany & Netherlands 10–4, Argentina 8–1, Mexico 6–0. So the fit pushes goal-sensitivity up and widens the host edge:

knobdefaultfitted to resultseffect
goal_elo_beta0.450.80favourites win bigger
home_advantage_elo80120hosts overperformed
avg_goals_per_team1.301.30unchanged
Fit error drops 22.5%, and modelled scoring moves from 2.73 → 3.03 goals/match, right onto the real 2.99. Higher β does most of the work: it lifts scoring and sharpens the favourites at once.

Now re-forecast the rest of the cup from the 32 teams that actually qualified, with the calibrated knobs. Uruguay, Senegal-as-favourite and other names are gone; the survivors' paths are fixed by the real bracket. The sharper model concentrates the odds at the top:

$ python3 calibrate.py --write # fit knobs to group results
$ python3 predict.py --actual --calibrated # re-forecast from the real R32

caveat Fitting three numbers to one group stage is a small sample — a few blowouts pull β upward, which is partly real (these are strong teams) and partly luck. That tension is exactly what the knobs let you probe: nudge β back toward 0.6 and see how much of Argentina's jump survives.

8 · Run it yourself

pure Python stdlib · no dependencies · ~2.5s for 20k sims

git clone https://github.com/fernfant/wc2026-predictor
cd wc2026-predictor

python3 predict.py # pre-tournament title race
python3 predict.py --matches # every group match's odds
python3 predict.py --full # stage-by-stage, all 48

python3 calibrate.py --write # fit knobs to actual group results
python3 predict.py --actual --calibrated # retrofit forecast from real R32

python3 sensitivity.py home_advantage_elo 0 80 160

Edit team ratings or the form column in data/teams.csv, tune the knobs in principles.py, and the forecast follows.

note The model predicts from the pre-tournament state. To re-forecast mid-event, prune the CSV to surviving teams.
